What is VBI?
RPI is fundamentally flawed because it values schedule strength more than winning itself. Since 75% of the formula is tied to opponents’ records and opponents’ opponents’ records, teams can climb the rankings with mediocre or even losing records simply by existing in power conferences. At that point, the system stops measuring performance and starts rewarding association and reputation.

VBI takes a different approach. It uses the Colley Matrix — a mathematically objective system of linear equations that evaluates every team simultaneously based only on game results. No polls. No preseason bias. No margin-of-victory inflation. The only variables are who you beat, who you lost to, and where the game was played. Instead of assuming conference strength, VBI forces teams to earn their rating through results on the field.
// the formula
C · r = b
Three things. That's the whole equation.
C  ·  r  =  b
C
the matrix
A 308×308 grid encoding every team's schedule. Diagonal = games played. Off-diagonal = negative games between those two teams.
r
the ratings
What we're solving for. One number per team. Higher = better. This is the VBI rating you see on the rankings page.
b
the record vector
One number per team encoding raw record: 1 + (W−L) / 2. A .500 team gets 1.5. An undefeated team gets higher.
The key insight: you can't solve for any one team's rating without knowing every other team's rating. So instead of going one team at a time, we write one equation per team and solve all 308 simultaneously. That's what makes it objective.
// home / away weighting
Location matters. A little.
Winning on the road is harder. The matrix accounts for that with a small multiplier on every game result.
×1.1
ROAD WIN
You won away from home. Your rating gets a 10% boost — and the home team's loss hurts 10% more.
×0.9
HOME WIN
You won at home. Expected. Slight discount — and the road team's loss hurts 10% less.
